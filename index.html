How to read this

A working draft

Built from what's known about Alma and Audrey's interests, skills, and history — meant to be marked up, argued with, and filled in. Two things are deliberate: the financials are illustrative placeholders, not projections; and the eight product categories are sequenced, not simultaneous. A two-person company cannot responsibly launch swimwear, jewelry, bags, purses, wallets, sweaters, hats, and flip-flops at once — so the plan stages them across three phases. That sequencing is the most important strategic choice here.

Executive summary

01Two athletes. Two makers. One house.

AddOne (stylized AAD1, pronounced “AddOne,” from Alma Audrey Division 1) is a direct-to-consumer active-lifestyle brand for design-literate women who move between water, mountains, and travel. It is founded by two former Division I athletes who are also working makers — Audrey in glass, ceramics, and design; Alma in ceramics and raku — who have already collaborated on two product lines (jewelry and ceramics).

The product universe spans the founders' own active life: swimwear and flip-flops (water), sweaters and knit hats (mountain), travel bags, purses, and wallets (carry), and jewelry (adornment). The company launches lean with the founders' proven, low-capital, high-margin categories, establishes brand and cash flow, then extends into the capital-intensive hero and lifestyle categories. The defensible edge is not any single product — it's the combination of athletic credibility, genuine making ability, apparel commercial experience, and a story competitors cannot copy.

Company & vision

02The rare middle

Mission. To make beautifully crafted, performance-minded gear and adornment for women who live actively — in the water, on the trail, and on the move.

Most active-women's brands are either technical (performance, no soul) or pretty (design, no credibility). AddOne sits in the rare middle: made by athletes who are also artists. The unifying thread across all eight categories is lifestyle, not material — the same woman from dawn swim to summit to airport.

“Heritage as a whisper, not a costume.”

Roles — shared & fungible, not fixed

Both founders are creatives; both design and make. Market & customer

04Who she is

Active, outdoorsy, design-conscious women, ~25–45, who swim, hike, and travel, have discretionary income, and prefer fewer, better, meaningful things. Essentially an older version of the founders and their peers — a strength, because they can design for someone they understand.

The honest part: every one of the eight categories is crowded and many are dominated by well-capitalized incumbents. AddOne does not win on specs or price. It wins on brand, story, and taste — which is exactly why brand and sequencing matter more than breadth.

Products — the universe & the rollout

05One brand, fired in stages

The eight categories are staged so the brand learns and earns before taking on capital-intensive product. Each tile below is a phase, not a product line to launch all at once.

01
Months 0–12 · Anchor

Prove it. Cheaply.

Jewelry (already made & sold together) + knit hats, optionally small leather goods. Low minimums, high margin, founder-made. Builds brand, audience, and cash before any technical product.

02
Months 12–24 · Hero

Swimwear.

The brand-defining product and Audrey's lived insight — deliberately not first, because it needs a technical designer, performance textiles, and inventory capital. Flip-flops follow alongside.

03
Months 24–36+ · Lifestyle

Round out the wardrobe.

Travel bags, purses, wallets, sweaters — leveraging the established brand, audience, and supplier relationships. Higher complexity and capital, taken on last.

The decisive fork

Make by hand, or design for manufacture? Founder-made (jewelry, ceramics-adjacent) is unique, high-margin, scarce, hard to scale. Manufactured (swimwear, footwear, bags, knitwear) is scalable but capital-intensive and supplier-dependent. Decide this per category — it governs capital, margins, and how much of the brand the founders physically touch.

Business model & pricing

08Scarcity by design

  • Channel: direct-to-consumer first (own the margin and the customer); selective wholesale only once established.
  • Inventory: made-to-order and limited drops early; deliberate small-batch scarcity (the Ferrari principle) turns the founders' biggest constraint into the strategy.
  • Pricing: premium and disciplined — never train customers to wait for discounts (the LVMH principle). Protect price; build value through craft, story, and scarcity.
  • Revenue: product sales (primary); limited capsules/collaborations; made-to-order jewelry; later, wholesale.

Operations & supply chain

09Coast to mountain

West Palm Beach (Audrey) and Joseph, Oregon (Alma) is a coast-and-mountain footprint that literally embodies the water-to-trail range — and a real operating spread (opposite corners of the country, a 3-hour gap, rural Joseph). Lean into it as a brand asset: each founder authentically anchors the categories native to her climate. But do not run inventory out of two homes 2,500 miles apart — centralize fulfillment or use a 3PL early.

Build vetted manufacturer relationships per category, lowest-MOQ first (knitwear, jewelry components), adding swimwear and leather as phases open. Define quality standards per line, resolving the tension between craft irregularity (beautiful, variable) and premium consistency. Clean books, separate banking, documented contributions from day one.

Risk analysis

12Where it breaks

  • Over-extension (the central risk). Eight categories is the diffusion trap. Mitigation: the phased rollout, and no new category until the prior phase is stable.
  • Capital intensity. Swimwear, leather, footwear need cash. Mitigation: sequence after cash-generating anchors.
  • Technical gap. Craft skill (ceramics/jewelry) doesn't transfer to swimwear/knit/footwear construction. Mitigation: hire/partner before those categories.
  • Coast-to-mountain coordination & fungible roles without accountability. Mitigation: centralized fulfillment, decision rhythms, and an explicit decision-ownership map.
  • Founder alignment & luxury-vs-craft consistency. Mitigation: partnership work first (and a post-mortem on the prior jewelry venture); define quality standards per line.
  • Brand/IP. Trademark and the “Division 1”/NCAA question cleared before public spend.

Key open decisions

13Resolve before building

  • Make-by-hand or design-for-manufacture — per category. Governs capital, margins, scale.
  • Lifestyle livelihood or scalable, sellable brand — shapes entity, funding, ambition.
  • Which category is truly first — the plan recommends jewelry + knit hats; the founders decide.
  • Equity split, vesting, buy-sell — including name rights on a founder's departure (the brand is named for them both).
  • Entity type and home state — live now that the founders sit in two states (FL / OR / Delaware); needs counsel.
  • Capital — how much, from where, and advisory structure — roles, equity/comp, and balancing the family-heavy bench.
